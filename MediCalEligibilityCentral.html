Members

The full synthetic roster. Click any row for the member record; click a column header to sort.

In plain EnglishEvery person here has a CIN — and yes, California calls it that too: the Client Index Number, eight digits and a letter, permanent through everything. This is the largest Medicaid program on earth — roughly one in three Californians — and its two defining facts organize this file. First, the county decides your kind of choice: in a COHS county there is one plan and everyone is in it automatically; in a Two-Plan county you pick between a public Local Initiative and a commercial; in a GMC county there is a menu. Second, since January 2024, full-scope Medi-Cal covers all income-eligible adults regardless of immigration status — register C holds the records that converted, aid code by aid code, the day the door opened.

Eligibility Spans

One row per coverage-group span — the state's answer to “from when to when, and under which door did this person enter?” Click a row to open the member.

In plain EnglishA span is a promise with dates on it — determined by one of 58 county welfare departments, all running on CalSAWS since the great three-system consolidation finished, and carried in California’s famous aid codes. Two eligibility facts here exist almost nowhere else: the R5 restricted-scope spans in register C, which ended by operation of law on January 1, 2024, when California completed full-scope coverage regardless of immigration status — and the non-MAGI rows, where since 2024 there is no asset test at all: California retired one of Medicaid’s oldest gatekeeping instruments entirely.

The Three Models

One row per delivery span — read the plan against its county’s model: teal rows are COHS plans (one plan, everyone, no choice), blue rows are the Two-Plan, GMC, and Regional counties (including L.A. Care and IEHP, the public Local Initiative giants), purple is Kaiser (the gated direct contract), gray is restricted-scope history and retro tails. Click a row to open the member.

In plain EnglishMedi-Cal never chose one managed care architecture — it chose three, by county, and kept them for decades. COHS (County Organized Health Systems, the oldest dating to 1983): one public plan, every member, automatic — zero choice by design. Two-Plan: a county-created public “Local Initiative” against one commercial — which is how L.A. Care became the largest publicly operated health plan in the nation. GMC: a genuine menu. The same program offers zero options in San Mateo, two in Los Angeles, and four in Sacramento — register D moves members across those lines and watches their kind of choosing change. Layered over all of it since 2024: Kaiser’s gated direct contract — the plan you cannot simply pick — and CalAIM, which moved the capitation’s boundary until it covered rent deposits and medically tailored meals.

What the File Says

California really does speak X12N 834 — DHCS’s roster files to the Medi-Cal plans, at the largest volumes in American Medicaid. Pick a member and read the transaction the plan would receive for that member's most recent enrollment event, segment by segment, with a plain-language note on every line.

In plain EnglishAn 834 is the state telling a health plan: this person is yours now (or isn't anymore) — and California’s stream carries two events worth studying. January 2024’s scope conversion was an addition wave with a strange property: hundreds of thousands of members who were already known to the state — years of restricted-scope history behind each CIN — arriving at plans as “new” enrollees. Every receiving plan’s duplicate-matching logic met its stress test that month. And register F’s files carry the matching rule in their silence: nothing in the 834 says the D-SNP must share the plan’s parent company — the alignment lives in state policy, one layer above the wire.
