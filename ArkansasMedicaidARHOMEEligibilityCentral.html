Members

The full synthetic roster. Click any row for the member record; click a column header to sort.

In plain EnglishEvery person here has a ten-digit ID that never changes — and the delivery column holds the strangest mix in the series. Arkansas never built ordinary Medicaid managed care. Its expansion adults are in commercial marketplace QHPs whose premiums Medicaid pays — the private option, the 2013 invention that let a deep-red legislature expand. Its most complex members are in PASSEs — provider-led entities holding global risk. And its kids and parents stayed in traditional fee-for-service. Three lanes, none of them a normal MCO. This is also the only state that ever implemented a Medicaid work requirement — register D holds what it left behind.

Eligibility Spans

One row per coverage-group span — the state's answer to “from when to when, and under which door did this person enter?” Click a row to open the member.

In plain EnglishA span is a promise with dates on it — and Arkansas’s spans include two kinds found nowhere else. Register C’s COFA cohort all begin the same month — Dec 2020, the restoration wall, where Congress ended a 24-year exclusion and a community enrolled at once. And register D carries closure reasons that a federal court later declared legally void: the 2018 work-requirement terminations, vacated by Gresham v. Azar — gaps whose stated cause is unlawful but whose uninsured months were lived anyway. A span table can hold a fact and its repudiation side by side; that is what makes it a record.

QHPs & PASSEs

One row per delivery span: a marketplace QHP (blue — the private option), a provider-led PASSE (purple), or traditional fee-for-service (gray). Click a row to open the member.

In plain EnglishArkansas answered "who delivers Medicaid?" three different ways for three different populations. Expansion adults: the marketplace — premium assistance into commercial QHPs at commercial rates, dearer than managed care but carrying commercial networks, and running on the marketplace’s own calendar (register B’s January plan switches inside unmoving eligibility — two clocks, one member). Complex members: PASSEs — provider-led entities, partly owned by the providers themselves, holding one global capitation for medical, behavioral, and developmental care, with tiers set by independent assessment. Everyone else: traditional fee-for-service, the lane the inventions never touched. No other state’s delivery column looks like this.

What the File Says

Arkansas really does speak X12N 834 — to marketplace carriers and PASSEs alike. Pick a member and read the transaction, segment by segment, with a plain-language note on every line.

In plain EnglishAn 834 is the state telling a health plan: this person is yours now — and in the private option the receiver is a commercial exchange carrier: the same Ambetter or Health Advantage that serves paying marketplace customers, receiving a Medicaid agency’s roster file. Register F’s exits are the series’ only 834s where the marketplace itself is the party being told goodbye. Fee-for-service members generate the state’s own record, shown in the same shape for teaching.
