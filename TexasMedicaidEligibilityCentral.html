In plain EnglishEvery person here has a nine-digit Medicaid ID that never changes. The roster shows why the state covers someone and who delivers the care — but in Texas the delivery answer has two levels: first a program, then a plan inside it. Which program is decided by who you are: families and kids ride STAR; adults with disabilities and seniors, STAR+PLUS; children with disabilities, STAR Kids — the only program of its kind in the nation; children in foster care, STAR Health. Eligibility Spans

One row per coverage-group span — the state's answer to “from when to when, and under which door did this person enter?” Click a row to open the member.

In plain EnglishA span is a promise with dates on it — opened through TIERS, the state’s integrated eligibility system, and closed for a reason. Texas adds an end reason this series shows nowhere else: closed at a periodic income check between renewals. Texas runs electronic data matches against wages mid-year, and a flag plus an unanswered ten-day letter can end a child’s span months before the renewal would have. And note what the parent rows imply: at roughly 16% FPL, Texas has the narrowest parent door and — with no expansion group — the largest coverage-gap population in the country.

The STAR Programs

One row per delivery span: which program (STAR, STAR+PLUS, STAR Kids, STAR Health) and which plan inside it — including the provider-owned plans children’s hospitals and county systems built — or traditional fee-for-service (retro tails). Click a row to open the member.

In plain EnglishTexas organized its managed care as a constellation of named programs, each with its own plan roster and its own rules for who belongs: STAR for families and children, STAR+PLUS for adults with disabilities and seniors — with long-term services and supports inside the capitation, which most Southern states never did — STAR Kids for children with disabilities (nobody else in the country has one), and STAR Health, a single statewide plan for every child in foster care. Two more Texas signatures ride the plan column: provider-owned plans — Texas Children’s, Parkland, Cook Children’s, Driscoll: the hospitals built themselves plans — and service areas: those plans exist only in their regions, so where you live shapes what you can pick. Program moves are life events; register D holds three of them.

What the File Says

Texas really does speak X12N 834 — at a volume few states match: HHSC’s enrollment broker and TMHP move rosters for millions of members across four programs and a dozen service areas. Pick a member and read the transaction the plan would receive for that member's most recent enrollment event, segment by segment, with a plain-language note on every line.

In plain EnglishAn 834 is the state telling a health plan: this person is yours now (or isn't anymore). In Texas, read the program alongside the plan: a STAR Kids termination paired with a STAR+PLUS addition on the same member is not churn — it is a 21st birthday, the most consequential transition in the file. A STAR termination paired with a STAR Health addition is a child entering foster care. The transactions are ordinary; the pairs tell the story. Systems that process 834s one at a time, without joining the goodbye to the hello, will see disruption where there is actually a designed hand-off.
Members are grouped by register; the transaction is rebuilt live from the member's actual spans.
