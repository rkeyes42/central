Members

The full synthetic roster. Click any row for the member record; click a column header to sort.

In plain EnglishEvery person here has an eight-digit Medicaid ID that never changes. The roster shows why the state covers someone and who delivers the care — one of three Medicaid Care Management plans statewide, or fee-for-service for the SSI-related and LTSS worlds. The chip to watch is GA02: Granite Advantage, the expansion group with a biography (it began life buying private marketplace plans) and a statutory trigger — if the federal match drops below 90%, the program ends by operation of law. Every open GA02 span in this file carries that contingency invisibly.

Eligibility Spans

One row per coverage-group span — the state's answer to “from when to when, and under which door did this person enter?” Click a row to open the member.

In plain EnglishA span is a promise with dates on it — determined in New Heights, applied for through NH EASY, and closed for a reason. New Hampshire’s file adds a rarer idea: promises with conditions attached at the statute level. Granite Advantage spans are real, open, and ordinary-looking — and their authorizing law contains a sunset that must be reauthorized and a federal-match trigger that could end every one of them at once. Register C splits down another statutory line: the waiver waives retroactive coverage for the expansion group, so two people applying the same day can get different backward reach depending on which door they came through.

Medicaid Care Management

One row per delivery span: which of the three statewide Medicaid Care Management plans held the member — AmeriHealth Caritas, NH Healthy Families, or WellSense — or fee-for-service for the SSI-related, LTSS, and dual populations. Click a row to open the member.

In plain EnglishNew Hampshire runs a compact version of the standard model: three MCOs, statewide, no regions — the state is small enough that geography never enters the plan question. The roster has one genuine original: WellSense, the plan of Boston Medical Center’s health system, provider-affiliated coverage that crosses the state line with its members. What the delivery model does not hold is as telling: the SSI-related, dual, and LTSS populations all sit outside Medicaid Care Management in fee-for-service — and the LTSS dollars carry a funding structure found almost nowhere else, because in New Hampshire the counties pay a share of long-term care and several of them run the nursing homes themselves. Register E is built on it.

What the File Says

New Hampshire really does speak X12N 834 — DHHS’s roster files to the three MCM plans. Pick a member and read the transaction the plan would receive for that member's most recent enrollment event, segment by segment, with a plain-language note on every line.

In plain EnglishAn 834 is the state telling a health plan: this person is yours now (or isn't anymore). New Hampshire’s file teaches by understatement. A Granite Advantage 021 looks like any other addition — nothing in the segments discloses that the coverage it grants sits under a statutory sunset and a federal-match trigger; the most consequential facts about a GA02 span live entirely outside the file, in a statute book. And register C’s additions split silently: the parent’s DTP*356 reaches backward, the expansion adult’s does not — same day, same office, different waiver clause. The lesson of this state’s EDI is that the file is never the whole record.
