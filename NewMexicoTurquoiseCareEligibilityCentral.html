Members

In plain EnglishEvery person here has a nine-digit Medicaid ID that never changes — which matters more here than anywhere, because in the summer of 2024 nearly everything around the ID changed at once: the program was renamed (Centennial Care became Turquoise Care), the agency was renamed (HSD became the Health Care Authority), one plan left, and one arrived. The person persisted; the labels moved. New Mexico also holds a superlative: Medicaid covers a larger share of the population here than in any other state — this roster is a sample of nearly half of New Mexico.

Eligibility Spans

One row per coverage-group span — the state's answer to “from when to when, and under which door did this person enter?” Click a row to open the member.

In plain EnglishA span is a promise with dates on it — and in New Mexico the promises are unusually load-bearing: with the nation’s highest Medicaid penetration, these spans are the health-coverage backbone of whole counties. Watch the LT01 rows: long-term services here are not a carve-out but the Community Benefit, delivered inside the plan capitation — and for 130 of register E’s members, self-directed: the member is the employer, the caregiver is often family, and a payroll runs inside the capitation. Register F’s end reason is worth reading closely too: Medicare arrives, the category changes — and the plan keeps the member.

Turquoise Care

One row per delivery span: which Turquoise Care plan held the member — BCBSNM, Presbyterian (provider-owned), Molina, or UnitedHealthcare — fee-for-service for Native members who opted out and retro tails, or the purple rows: Western Sky, the plan that left in June 2024. Click a row to open the member.

In plain EnglishNew Mexico integrated everything into the capitation back in 2014 — physical health, behavioral health, and long-term services in one plan payment, duals included — and Turquoise Care (July 2024) carried the design forward under a new name, a new agency (the Health Care Authority), and a reshuffled plan roster. That reshuffle is register D’s story and a first for this series: a plan exit. Western Sky lost the procurement and left; its members were scattered — some chose, some were auto-assigned — and every one of their timelines shows the purple ending in June 2024 for a reason no member controlled. Plans enter, plans convert, plans rebrand; this file shows what it looks like when one simply leaves.

What the File Says

New Mexico really does speak X12N 834 — the Health Care Authority’s roster files to the Turquoise Care plans. Note the sender: the same files said HSD before July 2024. Pick a member and read the transaction, segment by segment, with a plain-language note on every line.

In plain EnglishAn 834 is the state telling a health plan: this person is yours now (or isn't anymore). New Mexico’s file holds a transition every EDI architect eventually meets: the sender renamed itself. In July 2024 the program, the agency, and the trading-partner identifiers all changed while the members did not — a reminder that ISA envelopes are configuration, not truth, and that longitudinal member history must key on the person, never the sender. Register D adds the other half: a receiver that stopped existing. Between the renamed sender and the departed receiver, this file is a small museum of why identifiers need effective dates.
Members are grouped by register; the transaction is rebuilt live from the member's actual spans.
