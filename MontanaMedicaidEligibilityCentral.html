Eligibility Spans

One row per coverage-group span — the state's answer to “from when to when, and under which door did this person enter?” Click a row to open the member.

In plain EnglishA span is a promise with dates on it. It opens when a determination says yes, and it closes for a reason — a redetermination that didn't come back, an unpaid premium that outran its grace period, a move out of state. An open end date means the promise is still standing. Montana's spans carry the coverage group, because the group decides everything downstream: the benefit package, whether an invoice comes with it (HELP, the buy-in), and which rules end it.

HELP Premiums

One row per HELP expansion adult: the premium account behind the coverage. Invoiced monthly at roughly 2% of income; a 90-day grace period; disenrollment for nonpayment only above 100% FPL; AI/AN members exempt by law. Click a row to open the member.

In plain EnglishMontana said yes to expansion — and attached conditions. The HELP Act group carries a premium: a monthly invoice, roughly 2% of income, with real consequences. Miss it and a grace period runs; outrun the grace above 100% FPL and the case closes for nonpayment — a gap that paperwork never caused and a renewal never fixes, reopened only by payment (the arrears stay collectable, including by tax-refund offset). Below 100% FPL the invoice still arrives but the coverage cannot be taken for it; for AI/AN members it never arrives at all. The premium account is this app's second clock: not a plan enrollment, but a running status with dates — and it can end the eligibility span all by itself.

What the File Says

Montana has almost no plan to send an 834 to — and that is the honest lesson. For HMK children the separate CHIP's third-party administrator receives a real enrollment file; for everyone else, the rendering below is the enrollment record the state keeps for itself, shown in the standard 834 shape for teaching, segment by segment, with a plain-language note on every line.

In plain EnglishAn 834 is the state telling a health plan: this person is yours now (or isn't anymore). Montana mostly has no such plan — the state pays claims itself — so what you are reading is the record the state keeps about its own promise, dressed in the industry's standard shape. Watch the 2700 loop: that is where Montana's distinctive facts ride — the premium status, the AI/AN exemption, the IHS/638 facility whose services draw 100% federal match, T-HIP coordination, Medicare status. The interesting parts of a Montana record are conditions and funding, not plan assignments.
