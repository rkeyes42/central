In plain EnglishEvery person here has a ten-digit member ID that never changes: it survives gaps, plan switches, and island moves. The roster shows the usual two facts — why the state covers someone and who delivers the care — but in HawaiĘ»i the second column is nearly unanimous: everyone is in one of five QUEST Integration plans. Eligibility Spans

One row per coverage-group span — the state's answer to “from when to when, and under which door did this person enter?” Click a row to open the member.

In plain EnglishA span is a promise with dates on it. It opens when a determination says yes — through KOLEA, the eligibility system named for the Pacific golden plover — and it closes for a reason: a renewal that didn't come back, a move out of state. An open end date means the promise is still standing. Notice what does not end spans here: Medicare arriving (the plan keeps you), meeting nursing-facility level of care (LTSS is a rate cell, not a program), moving islands (the plan may change; the promise doesn't). HawaiĘ»i's eligibility axis is the calmest in the series because so much that convulses other states' records is absorbed by one integrated program.

QUEST Integration

One row per delivery span: which QUEST Integration plan held the member and under which rate cell — acute, LTSS at-risk, or dual — with fee-for-service appearing only as retro tails and processing slivers. Click a row to open the member.

In plain EnglishHawaiĘ»i is the everyone-in state: since 1994 the whole program has run as one 1115 demonstration, and since 2015 QUEST Integration has folded acute care, long-term services and supports, and even duals into the same five plans. So the interesting question here is never whether a member is in a plan — it is what changes underneath an enrollment that refuses to end. Watch the rate cell: the same plan span turns from acute to LTSS at-risk when nursing-facility level of care is met (HCBS opens; nobody moves plans), and to the dual rate when Medicare arrives (the plan keeps the member; only the payment changes). And watch the islands: Kaiser serves OĘ»ahu and Maui only, so a move to Hilo forces a plan change — the one state in this series where geography itself is a delivery event.

What the File Says

HawaiĘ»i really does speak X12N 834 — Med-QUEST's roster file to its five QUEST Integration plans, out of an MMIS platform it has long shared with Arizona. Pick a member and read the transaction the plan would receive for that member's most recent enrollment event, segment by segment, with a plain-language note on every line.

In plain EnglishAn 834 is the state telling a health plan: this person is yours now (or isn't anymore). In HawaiĘ»i the additions and terminations are the boring part — nearly everyone is added once and kept. The transactions that carry the money are the changes: a rate-cell move riding the 2700 loop when level of care is met or Medicare arrives, an island move re-drawing the service area, a COFA citizenship indicator. A plan that only reconciles who is on the roster — and not which cell each member sits in — will pay claims correctly and book revenue wrong all year.
Members are grouped by register; the transaction is rebuilt live from the member's actual spans.
