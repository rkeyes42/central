In plain EnglishEvery person here has an eleven-digit Nebraska Medicaid ID that never changes — and this file is a study in things that no longer exist but are still in the data: a benefit tier abolished after about a year (register C’s Basic/Prime ghost), a health plan that left the state (register D’s Healthy Blue succession, every span ending the same month), and a five-year waiting bar that expires into eligibility by pure date arithmetic (register E). Expansion here came by ballot initiative — November 2018 — and launched twenty-two months later, at the end of the longest runway in this series.

Eligibility Spans

One row per coverage-group span — the state's answer to “from when to when, and under which door did this person enter?” Click a row to open the member.

In plain EnglishA span is a promise with dates on it — and Nebraska’s file keeps three kinds of history most files never show. Register C’s launch cohort begins October 1, 2020: the wall at the end of Initiative 427’s twenty-two-month runway — a systems build, where Missouri’s wall was a court and Louisiana’s a go-live. Register C also carries tier spans: Basic/Prime, the benefit experiment abolished in October 2021 and preserved only as effective-dated rows. And register E’s bar-completers have begin dates equal to date of entry plus sixty months, exactly — eligibility as immigration arithmetic.

Heritage Health

One row per delivery span: Molina (the January 2024 successor), Nebraska Total Care, United — or Healthy Blue, present only as history, every one of its spans ending December 2023. Duals and tails sit in fee-for-service. Click a row to open the member.

In plain EnglishHeritage Health is Nebraska’s 2017 unified procurement: physical health, behavioral health, and pharmacy in one integrated capitation — an early full integrator on the series’ carve-out spectrum. The plan roster is a lesson in market motion: Healthy Blue exited December 2023 and Molina succeeded it on January 1, 2024, which is why every HB span in this file shares one termination date — a plan exit is a mass event, and the tell is thousands of strangers ending the same month. Register D reads the wave: the successor defaults, the members who chose otherwise during the transition window, and the quietest artifact of all — prior authorizations that outlived their plan, issued by a company that no longer operates here and binding on the one that does.

What the File Says

Nebraska really does speak X12N 834 — DHHS’s roster files to the Heritage Health plans. Pick a member and read the transaction the plan would receive for that member's most recent enrollment event, segment by segment, with a plain-language note on every line.

In plain EnglishAn 834 is the state telling a health plan: this person is yours now (or isn't anymore) — and Nebraska’s stream has carried some unusual freight. In January 2024 it carried a whole book of business at once: the Healthy Blue terminations and the Molina additions, paired, thousands deep, one shared effective date. In October 2021 it carried a mass benefit change with no enrollment event at all — the tier abolition, when every Basic member became Prime and no plan gained or lost a soul. And register E’s additions carry begin dates set by immigration arithmetic: entry-date-plus-sixty-months, computable years in advance.
Members are grouped by register; the transaction is rebuilt live from the member's actual spans.
