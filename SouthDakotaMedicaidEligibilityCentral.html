In plain EnglishEvery person here has an eight-digit South Dakota Medicaid ID that never changes — and the delivery column has exactly one value: fee-for-service, because South Dakota has no Medicaid managed care at all. This is the series’ second all-gray file — but where Alabama’s gray was the deliberate control group of a state that never expanded, South Dakota’s is gray and young: the newest expansion in the series (Amendment D, July 1, 2023) arrived with no plan layer whatsoever — tens of thousands of new adults, and not one 834 to a plan. Eligibility is the entire event, which makes this the cleanest place in the series to watch eligibility itself.

No Plans At All

One row per delivery span — and every one of them is fee-for-service, because South Dakota has no Medicaid MCO. What varies is the Health Home overlay: a per-member monthly care-coordination payment to an attributed clinic, with an acuity tier. Click a row to open the member.

In plain EnglishSouth Dakota pays every claim directly — no capitation, no plan network, no choice window, no 834 stream to plans — which makes it the natural experiment the rest of the series argues about: an expansion with no plan layer at all. The one managed-care-shaped structure is the Health Home: a clinic that agrees to be responsible for a chronically ill or seriously mentally ill member’s coordination, paid per member per month, with an acuity tier setting the rate. Register D reads it closely — attribution with a dial: Colorado’s app taught attribution as regional machinery, Alabama’s as the deliberate not-a-plan, and South Dakota adds tiering, where the payment follows the diagnosis’s weight. Attribution files with money on them get reconciled like enrollment rosters, because they are one.

What the File Says

South Dakota sends no 834 to any plan — there are none. What follows is the state’s own enrollment record, rendered in standard 834 shape for teaching: the same segments a plan-bound file would carry, addressed to the program itself. Pick a member and read it, segment by segment.

In plain EnglishAn 834 is the state telling a health plan: this person is yours now — and South Dakota has no one to tell. Every record below is the file the state keeps for itself: the same shape, the same segments, no receiver but the program. That absence is the teaching — everything the transaction normally negotiates (capitation start dates, plan assignment, switch seasons) simply is not here, and what remains is the eligibility core every other state’s 834 is wrapped around. Register E’s records carry the two facts that do the most work in this state: the tribal affiliation behind the 100% federal share, and — for the Oglala Lakota County cases — a county code whose own history requires an effective date.
