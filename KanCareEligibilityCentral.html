Members

The full synthetic roster. Click any row for the member record; click a column header to sort.

In plain EnglishEvery person here has a ten-digit Kansas Medicaid ID that never changes — and this file is Alabama’s exact mirror: both states unexpanded, holding opposite delivery answers. Alabama capitates no one; Kansas capitates nearly everyone — children, parents, the aged and disabled, duals, LTSS, and (nearly alone among states) the I/DD waivers themselves. So read the two columns against each other: the delivery column is almost solid plan; the group column never shows an expansion group, because none exists — the parent limit sits near 38% of poverty, and childless adults have no category at all. Everything capitated; nobody expanded.

Eligibility Spans

One row per coverage-group span — the state's answer to “from when to when, and under which door did this person enter?” Click a row to open the member.

In plain EnglishA span is a promise with dates on it — and Kansas’s spans end on the sharpest line in this series: the parent limit near 38% of poverty, low enough that ordinary wage noise crosses it (register C’s raise that cost coverage) and that the twelve-month postpartum clock runs out with nowhere to land — the span ends, and the record goes quiet. Register D’s spans hold the KanCare 3.0 turnover at this window’s exact left edge: every Aetna span ends December 31, 2024, every successor span begins January 1, 2025. And register F holds the series’ most inverted endings — including 130 members for whom an eligibility span opening at 65 is the whole story.

The All-In Capitation

One row per delivery span: Sunflower, United, Healthy Blue (the January 2025 arrival) — or Aetna, present only as history, every span ending December 31, 2024. Fee-for-service is rare here by design. Click a row to open the member.

In plain EnglishKanCare (2013) is the series’ maximal capitation: when Kansas moved to managed care it moved everything at once — children and parents, yes, but also the aged and disabled, full duals, nursing facility care, and, nearly alone among states, the home- and community-based waivers including I/DD. The plan is not a gatekeeper in front of the delicate populations; the plan is the delivery system for them — it holds the person-centered service plan, the attendant-care hours, the targeted case manager. Register E reads what that means: a waiver inside a capitation, a decade-long list preserved in two date fields, and members who are capitated and waitlisted at once. The paradox of this file is its frame: the most aggressive delivery modernization in the series, wrapped around eligibility rules with the lowest adult line in it.

What the File Says

Kansas really does speak X12N 834 — the state’s roster files to the KanCare plans. Pick a member and read the transaction the plan would receive for that member’s most recent enrollment event, segment by segment, with a plain-language note on every line.

In plain EnglishAn 834 is the state telling a health plan: this person is yours now (or isn't anymore) — and in Kansas “yours” means more than anywhere else in this series: the transaction hands over acute care, LTSS, and — for waiver members — someone’s entire daily support schedule. The January 2025 boundary carried a whole book of business: Aetna terminations and successor additions, paired, thousands deep, with waiver continuity freight attached. And register F’s QMB-only members generate no plan file at all — for them the record below is the state’s own, shown in standard shape: the first coverage in years, arriving at sixty-five.
Members are grouped by register; the transaction is rebuilt live from the member's actual spans.
