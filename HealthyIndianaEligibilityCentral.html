Members

The full synthetic roster. Click any row for the member record; click a column header to sort.

In plain EnglishEvery person here has a twelve-digit RID — the recipient ID — that never changes across three program brands: Hoosier Healthwise (kids and pregnancy), the Healthy Indiana Plan (the consumer-directed expansion), and Hoosier Care Connect (aged, blind, disabled). HIP is the reason this app exists: every HIP adult has a POWER account — a $2,500 ledger, mostly state-funded, topped by a monthly member contribution — and a tier that watches whether the payment arrives: Plus (paying: dental, vision, no copays) or Basic (not paying, below poverty: copays, thinner benefit). Nebraska proposed a tier system and walked away; Indiana has run one since 2015. The same four carriers serve all three brands.

Eligibility Spans

One row per coverage-group span — the state's answer to “from when to when, and under which door did this person enter?” Click a row to open the member.

In plain EnglishA span is a promise with dates on it — and Indiana attaches a ledger to the promise: HIP spans carry a POWER account whose contributions, balances, and rollovers move underneath the coverage without moving the dates. A missed payment below poverty doesn’t close the span — it drops the tier (register B’s drop-that-isn’t-a-closure), and register D’s life events flip members across program brands while the same carrier holds on. The span table tells you less of the story here than anywhere else in the series; the tier and the balance tell the rest.

Programs & Plans

One row per delivery span: which of the four carriers held the member — under Hoosier Healthwise, HIP, or Hoosier Care Connect — or fee-for-service for duals. Click a row to open the member.

In plain EnglishIndiana’s delivery landscape is simple on the carrier axis — Anthem, MDwise, MHS, and CareSource serve everything — and intricate on the program axis. HIP is the centerpiece: the 2015 deal that brought expansion to a red state by wrapping it in consumer direction — POWER accounts, contributions, tiers, and a decade of behavioral experiments (register E: work reporting died in court, lockouts were suspended, rollover rewards still run). Because the carriers span the brands, register D’s life-event flips — turning 19, reporting a pregnancy, a disability determination — change the member’s program, benefit, and financial relationship while the plan card in the wallet stays the same. Watch the program, not the plan.

Scenario Registers

Six business questions, each answered by a register of members whose data was planted to demonstrate exactly that scenario. Click any member to inspect the record behind the answer.

What the File Says

Indiana really does speak X12N 834 — the state’s roster files to its four carriers, across all three programs. Pick a member and read the transaction, segment by segment, with a plain-language note on every line.

In plain EnglishAn 834 is the state telling a health plan: this person is yours now — and Indiana’s files carry facts no other state’s do: the tier rides in a reporting loop (a Plus-to-Basic drop is a change record, not a termination — the quietest punishment in the series), and register F’s exit carries an amount: the POWER account’s closing settlement, the refund that makes the twenty-seventh answer the only goodbye with accounting. Fee-for-service duals generate the state’s own record, shown in the same shape for teaching.
Members are grouped by register; the transaction is rebuilt live from the member's actual spans.
