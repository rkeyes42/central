In plain EnglishEvery person here has a nine-digit North Dakota Medicaid ID that never changes — and the delivery column has exactly two values, because the state has exactly one plan: the expansion population is capitated to a single carrier (Sanford — provider-integrated at frontier scale), and everything else is fee-for-service. No menu, no market, no auto-assignment lottery: the enrollment question in North Dakota has one answer, unless you belong to the one group that can decline it — register E’s AI/AN members, whose exemption is the only enrollment choice in the state. Every phone number in this file shares one area code, because the state does too.

Eligibility Spans

One row per coverage-group span — the state's answer to “from when to when, and under which door did this person enter?” Click a row to open the member.

In plain EnglishA span is a promise with dates on it — and this file’s end reasons carry the Bakken: register C’s oil-patch members close over income — rig employment at the spring thaw and reopen at freeze-up. That is not paperwork churn; the renewal came back fine — the wages did it, on a seasonal cycle the annual-renewal model was never designed for. Behind every determination sits one of nineteen human service zones, the 2020 consolidation of forty-six county offices — register D’s administrative-geography story, told in office-of-record codes.

The One Plan

One row per delivery span — and only two possible values: Sanford Health Plan (the expansion population’s single carrier, the only Medicaid plan in the state) or fee-for-service (everyone else, which is most of the program). Click a row to open the member.

In plain EnglishNorth Dakota built the smallest possible managed care program: one population, one plan. The expansion adults — and only they — are capitated, to a single statewide carrier procured without a competitive market to speak of; children, families, the aged and disabled, and all of LTSS stay fee-for-service. Every mechanism eighteen other apps have taught — choice windows, auto-assignment algorithms, plan switching, market-share balancing — does not exist here, because they all require a second plan. The 834 stream has one receiver. The one exception proves the design: AI/AN expansion adults hold a federal exemption and may take fee-for-service instead — the only enrollment choice in the state, held by exactly one population, and register E shows it exercised in both directions.

What the File Says

North Dakota really does speak X12N 834 — to precisely one receiver. Pick a member and read the transaction, segment by segment, with a plain-language note on every line.

In plain EnglishAn 834 is the state telling a health plan: this person is yours now (or isn't anymore) — and in North Dakota, “a health plan” is not a category but a proper noun: every plan-bound transaction in the state goes to the same trading partner. One receiver means no plan-to-plan switches, no competing auto-assignments, no market-share balancing — the maintenance-code vocabulary shrinks to additions, terminations, and changes against a single relationship. Register F’s terminations carry the state’s quietest irony: the member the plan releases at 65 is very often enrolled, weeks later, by the same health system’s Medicare side — a catch this stream cannot see.
