Members

The full synthetic roster. Click any row for the member record; click a column header to sort.

In plain EnglishEvery person here has a ten-digit recipient ID that never changes — and every person here is split between two governments. Physical health rides a zone-based plan the state procured; behavioral health rides a capitation the member’s county holds — in Philadelphia, a plan that is literally a city department. The delivery column shows the physical half; the county behavioral contractor rides along in each member’s detail, constant as long as the county is. Pennsylvania is the only state in this series that splits one member between two levels of government — and this app is about reading that seam.

One row per coverage-group span — the state's answer to “from when to when, and under which door did this person enter?” Click a row to open the member.

In plain EnglishA span is a promise with dates on it — and in Pennsylvania one eligibility span feeds two capitations at once: the state’s zone plan and the county’s behavioral contractor draw on the same determination, so when register B’s renewals fail, both halves pause together. Register C carries the other signature: Community HealthChoices launched in waves — Southwest 2018, Southeast 2019, everywhere else 2020 — so its start dates form a gradient across the map rather than a single wall.

The Carve

One row per physical-health delivery span — zone plans and Community HealthChoices. The behavioral half is a second, county-held capitation shown in each member’s detail: constant while the county is, indifferent to everything that moves these rows. Click a row to open the member.

In plain EnglishPennsylvania runs the carve: in the 1990s, when HealthChoices was built, the behavioral-health dollar was carved out of the physical capitation and offered to county governments first — the right of first opportunity — and the counties took it, everywhere. So every member carries two capitations held by two levels of government: a state-procured zone plan for physical health, and a county-held behavioral contractor — Philadelphia’s is CBH, an actual city department. The county side keeps its savings and must reinvest them locally (register E’s housing programs and crisis teams), and it is indifferent to Medicare (register F’s third answer). Register D shows the seam in motion: a county move can switch half the member while the other half never blinks.

What the File Says

Pennsylvania really does speak X12N 834 — to its zone plans, its CHC plans, and the county behavioral contractors alike. Pick a member and read the transaction, segment by segment, with a plain-language note on every line.

In plain EnglishAn 834 is a government telling a health plan: this person is yours now — and in Pennsylvania two governments say it about the same person. The state’s stream feeds the zone plans and CHC; the county side maintains its own roster of the same members for the behavioral capitation, carried here as an LX loop on every file. Register F’s birthday generates the fullest single month in the series: a 024 to one plan, a 021 to another, and — on the county side — nothing at all, which is itself the lesson.
Members are grouped by register; the transaction is rebuilt live from the member's actual spans.

All members, identifiers, addresses, phone numbers, and dates are synthetic and deterministic (seeded PRNG, seed 215001); any resemblance to real persons is coincidental. Program facts reflect public Pennsylvania Medical Assistance policy of the mid-2020s and change year to year — the zone map, county BH assignments, and CHC mechanics are teaching simplifications; verify against current PA DHS and CMS publications before any non-demonstration use.
