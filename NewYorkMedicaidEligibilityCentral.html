In plain EnglishEvery person here has a CIN — the Client Identification Number, two letters, five digits, one letter — that never changes: it survives gaps, plan switches, borough moves, and product-line changes. The roster shows two different facts side by side: why the state covers someone (the coverage group) and who delivers the care today — a mainstream plan, a HARP, an MLTC plan, or fee-for-service. Those are separate columns because they are separate systems.

Eligibility Spans

One row per coverage-group span — the state's answer to “from when to when, and under which door did this person enter?” Click a row to open the member.

In plain EnglishA span is a promise with dates on it. It opens when a determination says yes, and it closes for a reason — a recertification that didn't come back, a move onto Medicare, a move out of state. An open end date means the promise is still standing. New York's spans carry the coverage group, because the group decides everything downstream: which managed care products the member can (or must) enroll in, what the benefit package looks like, and which rules end it.

Managed Care Enrollment

One row per delivery-system span: which plan held the member — and under which product line (mainstream, HARP, MLTC) — or whether the member sat in fee-for-service. Click a row to open the member.

In plain EnglishA delivery span is not just a plan name — the plan is the value; the span is that value held over a date range, with a reason it began and ended. In New York the value comes in four kinds: a mainstream plan (full capitation), a HARP (a mainstream plan's enhanced behavioral-health product line), an MLTC plan (partial capitation — the plan is paid for long-term services and supports only, while Medicare stays primary for medical), or FFS (no plan; the state paying claims directly — a positive fact with its own dates, not a gap). Eligibility and delivery run on two different clocks: eligibility can be granted backward, but plan enrollment only runs forward, from the first of a month. That is why a member's history can honestly read plan → FFS → plan — and why a dual entering MLTC shows a new plan span while the eligibility span never blinks.

What the File Says

New York really does speak X12N 834 — the state's roster file to its managed care plans, mainstream and MLTC alike. Pick a member and read the transaction the plan would receive for that member's most recent enrollment event, segment by segment, with a plain-language note on every line.

In plain EnglishAn 834 is the state telling a health plan: this person is yours now (or isn't anymore). It carries who the person is, where they live, when coverage starts or stops, and — in New York's file — the coverage group and restriction/exception facts that explain why. For an MLTC plan the same transaction means something narrower: yours for long-term services and supports only. Fee-for-service members generate no plan 834 at all: for them, the file below is the enrollment record the state keeps for itself, shown in the same shape for teaching.
Members are grouped by register; the transaction is rebuilt live from the member's actual spans.
