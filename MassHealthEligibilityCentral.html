Coverage Types & Spans

One row per coverage-group span — the state's answer to “from when to when, and under which door did this person enter?” Click a row to open the member.

In plain EnglishA span is a promise with dates on it — but a MassHealth span promises a rung, not just a yes. It opens when a determination lands someone on Standard, CarePlus, CommonHealth, Family Assistance, or Limited, and it closes for a reason: an annual review that didn't come back, a move to a different rung (the end reason this series sees nowhere else), a sixty-fifth birthday ending CarePlus by rule. Watch for pairs of adjacent spans with no gap between them — that is the ladder moving under a member while their coverage, and usually their plan, never blinks.

ACOs & Managed Care

One row per delivery-system span: which plan held the member — an ACO (the flagship: a plan built around a provider system), an MCO, the state-run PCC plan (behavioral health via MBHP), a One Care or SCO integrated dual plan — or fee-for-service. Click a row to open the member.

In plain EnglishMassachusetts reorganized its managed care around the people who actually deliver the care: since 2018 the flagship is the ACO — a plan and a provider system fused, so “who is your plan” and “who is your doctor” point at the same institution. Around the ACOs sit traditional MCOs, the state-run PCC plan with behavioral health carved out to MBHP, and — the state’s pride — the integrated dual plans: One Care for duals under 65 and SCO for 65 and over, each putting Medicare and Medicaid behind one card. And here is the Massachusetts inversion worth staring at: in register D the eligibility axis moves (a rung change) while the delivery span runs straight through, unbroken — the exact mirror image of every state where the plan changed and eligibility held still.

What the File Says

MassHealth really does speak X12N 834 — the roster file to its ACOs, MCOs, One Care and SCO plans, and the MBHP behavioral carve-out. Pick a member and read the transaction the plan would receive for that member's most recent enrollment event, segment by segment, with a plain-language note on every line.

In plain EnglishAn 834 is the state telling a health plan: this person is yours now (or isn't anymore). In Massachusetts the segment to stare at is in the 2700 reporting loop: the coverage type. When a member moves rungs, the plan receives a change — not a termination, not an addition — and the same enrollment continues at a different rate cell with a different benefit behind it. A plan that misreads a rung change as a disenrollment invents churn that never happened; one that ignores it pays for the wrong benefit package. Limited members generate no plan 834 at all — for them the rendering below is the state's own record in the standard shape.
