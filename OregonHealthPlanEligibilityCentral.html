Members

The full synthetic roster. Click any row for the member record; click a column header to sort.

In plain EnglishEvery person here has an Oregon Health Plan ID — two letters, six digits — that never changes. The delivery column holds coordinated care organizations: not insurers that won bids but regional collaboratives with community boards, each holding one global budget for physical, behavioral, and oral health. Assignment is geography — the CCO map is a map of communities, and most counties have exactly one. And Oregon’s eligibility side carries two national firsts: continuous eligibility to age six for young children and a two-year clock for adults — register C reads what a span with no renewals in it looks like.

Eligibility Spans

One row per coverage-group span — the state's answer to “from when to when, and under which door did this person enter?” Click a row to open the member.

In plain EnglishA span is a promise with dates on it — and Oregon changed what the promise sounds like. A child under six holds one span from birth to the sixth birthday with no renewals in it: nothing to miss, nothing to churn on. Adults run a 24-month clock instead of the annual one every other state in this series uses. Register B still shows churn — for those on the old clock — and the comparison between B’s gaps and C’s unbroken bars is the same population under two different renewal policies: the clearest natural experiment in the series.

The CCOs

One row per delivery span: which coordinated care organization held the member — assignment by county, community governance, one global budget each — or Open Card fee-for-service for duals and exemptions. Click a row to open the member.

In plain EnglishOregon’s 2012 invention is a third answer to the question every other app in this series poses two ways. Not fee-for-service, not competing statewide insurers — a coordinated care organization: one entity per region, governed by a board where local providers, county government, and community members sit together, paid one global budget covering physical, behavioral, and oral health, with a legislated growth cap. Because the budget is global, the CCO can spend it where health actually happens — register E’s air conditioners, rent assistance, and doulas are claims in this file. And because assignment is geography, the choice machinery the rest of the series teaches barely runs here: the menu exists only where regions overlap, and register D’s Lane County members are nearly the only choosers in the state.

What the File Says

Oregon really does speak X12N 834 — the state’s roster files to its CCOs. Pick a member and read the transaction, segment by segment, with a plain-language note on every line.

In plain EnglishAn 834 is the state telling a health plan: this person is yours now — and in Oregon “yours” means yours as a community: the receiver is a regional collaborative, and the addition carries a person into a global budget rather than a rate cell. Register C’s additions are the quietest in the series — begin dates with no renewal cycle behind them, some fixed years out by a sixth birthday. Register E’s members generate encounters no other state’s stream carries: an air conditioner with a service code, six months of rent with a delivery date.
Members are grouped by register; the transaction is rebuilt live from the member's actual spans.

All members, identifiers, addresses, phone numbers, and dates are synthetic and deterministic (seeded PRNG, seed 503001); any resemblance to real persons is coincidental. Program facts reflect public Oregon Health Authority policy of the mid-2020s and change year to year — the CCO service-area map and benefit mechanics are teaching simplifications; verify against current OHA and CMS publications before any non-demonstration use.
