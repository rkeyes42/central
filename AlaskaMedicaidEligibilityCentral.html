The shape of Alaska Medicaid

Most state Medicaid programs hand their members to capitated managed-care plans and let the plans worry about them. Alaska never did. Nearly every covered Alaskan is straight fee-for-service: the state's fiscal agent pays claims directly, eligibility is the whole ballgame, and there is no plan enrollment file because there are no plans. What Alaska has instead is geography — and a second, sovereign delivery system, the Alaska Tribal Health System, whose services for IHS-eligible members draw a 100% federal match.

In plain English
In most states the hard question is “which plan is this member in?” In Alaska the hard questions are “is this person eligible right now, under which category, and are they an IHS beneficiary?” — because those three answers decide who pays, how much, and whether the federal government pays all of it.

Why this app exists

The app models a synthetic recipient population over a 24-month window (Jan 2025 – Dec 2026) with the events that actually drive Alaska eligibility work: renewal churn, three-month retroactive grants, children aging out of Denali KidCare, tribal-facility affiliation, and Medicare duals waiting on buy-in accretion. Every member is generated from a fixed seed, so the data is identical on every load — discuss a member by ID and everyone sees the same story.

All members, identifiers, and category codes are synthetic and illustrative. Program facts reflect publicly known Alaska Medicaid policy of the mid-2020s and should be verified against current Alaska Department of Health publications before any non-demonstration use.

Eligibility spans

Every dated segment of coverage across the population, one row per span. Open-ended spans are eligibility that is still in force as of the July 2026 “today” line. Retro spans begin before their own application date — Alaska, like most states, can grant up to three months of retroactive coverage when the person would have been eligible.

In plain English
A span is a promise with dates on it: between this day and that day, the program pays. Fee-for-service means there is no plan downstream to catch a mistake — if the span is wrong, the claim payment is wrong.

What the file says

California mails its counties a proprietary, published flat file and everyone reads from the same layout document. Alaska has no public equivalent — the state’s fiscal agent and its trading partners move eligibility the way the rest of the industry does, on the ASC X12N 834 (005010X220A1 Benefit Enrollment and Maintenance). So this tab reads the 834: pick a member and see their eligibility story rendered as the segments a downstream system would actually parse, each one annotated.

In plain English
An 834 is a sentence diagram for coverage: the INS line says “here is a person and what to do with them,” the REF lines say who they are, the HD lines say what coverage, and the DTP lines say when. Everything the timeline draws in color, the file says in asterisks.

Teaching rendering, not a companion guide: envelope IDs, qualifier choices (e.g., the mutually-defined REF*ZZ tribal-affiliation flag and the category code carried in HD04), maintenance codes, and addresses are illustrative. Real trading-partner implementations pin these down in a companion guide, and the guide always wins. Members render as a single-member verification transaction for readability.

In plain English
The category code is the door the person came through, and in Alaska the door also decides the money: services an IHS-eligible member receives “through” a tribal facility are matched at 100% by the federal government, so a single affiliation flag can move the state’s share of a claim from roughly half to zero.

Denali KidCare is Alaska’s branding for children’s and pregnancy coverage (Medicaid plus CHIP). The Adult Public Assistance (APA) linkage for aged, blind, and disabled categories, the Group VIII expansion population, and the three-month retroactive window are described at the policy level in the companion Business Explanations document.
