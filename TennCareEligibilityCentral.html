Members

The full synthetic roster. Click any row for the member record; click a column header to sort.

In plain EnglishEvery person here has a nine-digit ID that never changes — and a plan, always: on January 1, 1994, Tennessee moved its entire Medicaid program into capitated managed care in one leap, a decade before any other state dared, and there has been no fee-for-service lane to fall into since. Three MCOs — BlueCare, Wellpoint, UnitedHealthcare — carry everything: kids, CoverKids CHIP, pregnancy, CHOICES long-term care, I/DD supports, duals. Tennessee never expanded, so the adult doors are narrow; what it built instead is the series’ oldest managed-care machine, now running under TennCare III’s aggregate spending cap — and, in register B, the renewal system a federal court took apart.

Eligibility Spans

One row per coverage-group span — the state's answer to “from when to when, and under which door did this person enter?” Click a row to open the member.

In plain EnglishA span is a promise with dates on it — and TennCare’s spans include the series’ extremes of both age and repair. Register C’s long-tenure members have open spans that began in the 1990s — coverage older than the internet, from the 1994 big bang’s first generation. And register B carries an end reason unique to this state: “reinstated retroactively (A.M.C. v. Smith)” — closures a federal court found unlawful and ordered backfilled, so the spans sit contiguous with the gap erased. Most unwinding churn was measured by researchers; Tennessee’s was adjudicated, and the remedy is visible as a reason code.

MCO Enrollment

One row per delivery span: which of the three MCOs held the member. There is no fee-for-service lane — total managed care since 1994. Click a row to open the member.

In plain EnglishTennCare’s delivery table is the simplest in the series to read and the heaviest in what it carries. Three MCOs hold everything: medical care, behavioral health, CHOICES nursing-facility and home-care benefits, Employment and Community First I/DD supports, CoverKids CHIP children, and the Medicaid side of every dual — one capitation per member, no carve-outs, no FFS lane. That totality is what makes register F’s answer possible: because the MCO never lets go at Medicare, the state could write alignment into its procurement — requiring each TennCare plan to run a companion D-SNP and steering new duals into the same parent. The delivery span barely moves in this app; the weight it bears is the story.

What the File Says

TennCare really does speak X12N 834 — the state’s roster files to its three MCOs. Pick a member and read the transaction, segment by segment, with a plain-language note on every line.

In plain EnglishAn 834 is the state telling a health plan: this person is yours now — and TennCare’s register F files are notable for what they do not contain: no termination. The MCO keeps the member through the Medicare birthday; the transaction is a change record carrying the Medicare status — and, for aligned members, the fact that the same corporate parent’s D-SNP now holds the other half. Register B’s reinstatements carry the court’s fingerprint: a retroactive begin date backfilling an erased gap.
Members are grouped by register; the transaction is rebuilt live from the member's actual spans.
