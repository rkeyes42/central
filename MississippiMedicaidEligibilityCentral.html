Members

The full synthetic roster. Click any row for the member record; click a column header to sort.

In plain EnglishEvery person here has a nine-digit Medicaid ID that never changes: it survives gaps, plan switches, and county moves. The roster shows the usual two facts — why the state covers someone and who delivers the care — and the first column tells the Mississippi story before you open a single record: it is mostly children. Six of ten members are kids; most of the rest are pregnant or postpartum women, people with disabilities, and elders. What you will not find is an adult expansion group — it does not exist here — and register D shows what that absence does to a record. Watch the Closed chips: in this state, some of them are not churn.

Eligibility Spans

One row per coverage-group span — the state's answer to “from when to when, and under which door did this person enter?” Click a row to open the member.

In plain EnglishA span is a promise with dates on it. It opens when a determination says yes, and it closes for a reason — and Mississippi's end reasons include three this series shows nowhere else: postpartum period complete, youngest child turned 18, and income above the parent limit and below the marketplace floor. In an expansion state, each of those events would be a group change — one span ending, another beginning the next day. Here they are closures, because the group they would move to does not exist. Read the reason column carefully in this state: it is where the policy lives.

MississippiCAN

One row per delivery span: which coordinated care organization (CCO) held the member — Magnolia, Molina, or TrueCare — or whether the member sat in fee-for-service: duals, LTSS, AI/AN members who opted out, and retro tails. Click a row to open the member.

In plain EnglishSince 2011 MississippiCAN (the Mississippi Coordinated Access Network) has put most children, families, and pregnant members into one of three CCOs — including TrueCare, a plan owned by Mississippi's own hospitals, a detail worth pausing on in a state where rural hospitals are fighting to stay open: the providers built themselves a plan. Around the CCOs sits the fee-for-service remnant — duals and LTSS members are excluded from MississippiCAN, AI/AN members may opt out (the Choctaw Health Center is an IHS medical home), and retro tails run FFS everywhere. The delivery axis here is conventional; Mississippi's drama is on the eligibility axis, in what ends and never resumes.

What the File Says

Mississippi really does speak X12N 834 — the Division of Medicaid's roster file to its three CCOs, issued from MESA, the Medicaid Enterprise System that replaced the legacy MMIS in 2022. Pick a member and read the transaction the plan would receive for that member's most recent enrollment event, segment by segment, with a plain-language note on every line.

In plain EnglishAn 834 is the state telling a health plan: this person is yours now (or isn't anymore). Mississippi's file looks like every other state's — same segments, same codes — and that is exactly the point of register D: a coverage-gap closure and an ordinary churn closure produce byte-identical 024s. The transaction cannot say “this one is coming back” or “this one has nowhere to go.” Only the eligibility record's reason — and whether anything ever follows — tells the two apart. Fee-for-service members generate no plan 834 at all: for them, the rendering below is the state's own record in the standard shape.
Members are grouped by register; the transaction is rebuilt live from the member's actual spans.

All members, identifiers, addresses, phone numbers, and dates are synthetic and deterministic (seeded PRNG, seed 601001); any resemblance to real persons is coincidental.
