Members

The full synthetic roster. Click any row for the member record; click a column header to sort.

In plain EnglishEvery person here has an eight-digit PMI — the Person Master Index number — that never changes. Two things make this roster unlike any other in the series. First, the group column includes MinnesotaCare: the Basic Health Program, Medicaid’s upstairs neighbor — when income rises past 138%, coverage converts instead of ending. Second, the plan shelf includes governments: Hennepin Health is owned by a county; South Country Health Alliance is owned jointly by rural county boards — and every other carrier is a nonprofit, because Minnesota long barred for-profit HMOs. Products (PMAP, SNBC, MSC+, MSHO) ride on the plans by life stage — and MSHO holds Medicare and Medicaid in one plan at once.

Eligibility Spans

One row per coverage-group span — the state's answer to “from when to when, and under which door did this person enter?” Click a row to open the member.

In plain EnglishA span is a promise with dates on it — and Minnesota’s spans include an end reason almost no other state can write: “converted — no gap.” When income crosses the 138% line, Medical Assistance closes and MinnesotaCare opens the next day — register B’s net, register C’s two-way ladder. Kansas’s register C showed what the cliff does to a record; this app shows what the anti-cliff looks like: contiguous spans, program labels flipping, coverage never blinking. Churn measured on coverage is zero; churn measured on program is not — choose your metric carefully.

Plans & Products

One row per delivery span: which plan held the member, and under which product — PMAP, SNBC, MSC+, or MSHO (the integrated dual product) — or fee-for-service under the tribal election. Click a row to open the member.

In plain EnglishMinnesota’s shelf is unlike any other in the series twice over. Who owns the plans: every carrier is a nonprofit or a government — including Hennepin Health (owned by a county) and South Country Health Alliance (owned by rural county boards under joint powers): county government competing as a carrier, on a menu, which is Oregon’s community governance and Philadelphia’s public plan rolled into a market. What rides on them: products by life stage — PMAP for families and working-age adults, SNBC for adults with disabilities, and at 65 the fork this whole app builds toward: MSHO, where one plan takes Medicare and Medicaid together (the oldest integrated dual product in the country, 1997), or MSC+, the Medicaid-only path for those who decline. Register F is that fork, member by member.

What the File Says

Minnesota really does speak X12N 834 — the state’s roster files to its plans, across every product. Pick a member and read the transaction, segment by segment, with a plain-language note on every line.

In plain EnglishAn 834 is the state telling a health plan: this person is yours now — and Minnesota’s quietest files are its best ones: register B and C’s conversions often produce no plan transaction at all, because the member kept the same plan while the program label flipped underneath — the anti-cliff leaves almost no wake. Register F’s aligned members produce a product change to the same carrier: the 834 equivalent of an upgrade letter. Tribal-election members generate the state’s own record, shown in the same shape for teaching.
Members are grouped by register; the transaction is rebuilt live from the member's actual spans.
