Members

The full synthetic roster. Click any row for the member record; click a column header to sort.

In plain EnglishEvery person here has an eight-digit DCN — the Departmental Client Number — that never changes, and one fact organizes the whole file: Missouri’s delivery boundary runs through the caseload, not the map. Managed care is statewide (since May 2017) — but only for the MAGI populations: children, families, pregnant women, and the Amendment 2 expansion adults. The aged, blind, and disabled side is fee-for-service. Whether a Missourian has a plan depends on who they are, not where they live — Nevada’s app drew this line with a road atlas; Missouri draws it with a category code, and register D crosses it in both directions.

Eligibility Spans

One row per coverage-group span — the state's answer to “from when to when, and under which door did this person enter?” Click a row to open the member.

In plain EnglishA span is a promise with dates on it — and this file contains the only begin date in the Central series that was set by a court: July 1, 2021, the effective date the voters wrote into the Missouri Constitution, the legislature refused to fund, and the state Supreme Court enforced. Register C’s backdated cohort all share it. Read the end reasons closely too: in Missouri, a disability determination and a Medicare entitlement are not just eligibility events — because the delivery boundary is categorical, they are automatically delivery events as well. And register F’s SD01 spans stay open only as long as a monthly pay-in arrives: eligibility as a subscription.

The Population Line

One row per delivery span: one of the three MO HealthNet Managed Care plans — Home State Health (Centene, headquartered in St. Louis), Healthy Blue, UnitedHealthcare — the purple Show Me Healthy Kids specialty plan for foster care, or fee-for-service: the ABD side of the population line. Click a row to open the member.

In plain EnglishMissouri splits its program by population: MAGI groups (children, families, pregnant women, Amendment 2 adults, foster youth) ride statewide managed care; the aged, blind, and disabled ride fee-for-service — including the state’s LTSS. The consequence is the register D teaching: category changes are delivery changes. A disability determination ends a plan; an age-18 redetermination that finds the adult standard unmet creates one; a Medicare entitlement, which moves the category to the ABD side, terminates the plan automatically — the seventeenth answer in this series’ Medicare catalog, and the first produced entirely by a category code. One more Missouri original rides above the line: Show Me Healthy Kids, the statewide specialty plan for every child in foster care or adoption subsidy — one plan, automatic, everywhere, with enrollment dates that trace to court orders rather than applications.

What the File Says

Missouri really does speak X12N 834 — MO HealthNet’s roster files to its managed care plans, Show Me Healthy Kids included. Pick a member and read the transaction the plan would receive for that member's most recent enrollment event, segment by segment, with a plain-language note on every line.

In plain EnglishAn 834 is the state telling a health plan: this person is yours now (or isn't anymore) — and Missouri’s stream carries terminations worth filing carefully. Register D’s and F’s 024s fire for members whose address, income need, and clinic never changed: the category crossed the population line — by diagnosis, by redetermination, by birthday — and the plan relationship ended because the caseload boundary said so. Show Me Healthy Kids receives the series’ strangest begin dates: enrollment effective the day a court ordered custody. And register F’s pay-in members generate no plan file at all — their signature transaction runs the other direction: a monthly payment from the member to the state, holding the eligibility span open.
Members are grouped by register; the transaction is rebuilt live from the member's actual spans.
