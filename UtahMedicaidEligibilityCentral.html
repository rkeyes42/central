Members

The full synthetic roster. Click any row for the member record; click a column header to sort.

In plain EnglishEvery person here has a ten-digit Utah Medicaid ID that never changes — and every active member here carries two capitations at once. Physical health goes to one of four ACOs (Utah’s statutory word for a full-risk Medicaid plan — a vocabulary collision register readers from Medicare-land should savor), inside the four Wasatch Front counties. Behavioral health is carved out to the county governments’ mental health authorities — the PMHPs — statewide: the carve-out has no city limit. The delivery column below shows the physical lane; the behavioral capitation rides on every record as a second relationship the roster must remember to ask about.

Eligibility Spans

One row per coverage-group span — the state's answer to “from when to when, and under which door did this person enter?” Click a row to open the member.

In plain EnglishA span is a promise with dates on it — determined, in Utah, by the Department of Workforce Services: the workforce agency runs Medicaid eligibility. Two things to read closely here. First, the sediment: register C’s adult group codes date their members’ arrivals like tree rings — TAM (the 2017 targeted waiver), B96 (the 2019 bridge, now existing only as closed spans), AE (the 2020 fallback). Second, the short reach-back: Utah’s waiver restricts retroactive coverage for most adults to the application month itself — no three-month tail. Eighteen apps taught the classic retro; this file is where the reach-back nearly vanishes.

ACOs & the Carve-Out

One row per physical-lane span: one of the four ACOs — SelectHealth (Intermountain), Healthy U (the University of Utah), Health Choice, Molina — or fee-for-service outside the Wasatch Front. The behavioral lane — the county PMHP capitation — rides on every member and is shown in the member modal; these span tables draw one of the two lanes. Click a row to open the member.

In plain EnglishUtah’s delivery model is a deliberate split. Physical health: four full-risk plans the statute calls ACOs — and note the collision: in Medicare, an ACO is an attribution-and-shared-savings arrangement; in Utah Medicaid, it is a capitated MCO, full stop. Behavioral health: carved out to county governments — the local mental health and substance abuse authorities, each holding a prepaid capitation (the PMHP) for its county’s residents, with Salt Lake County contracting a national administrator to run its book. Washington’s app in this series shows a state that spent 2016–2020 killing this design; Utah runs it on purpose, which makes every member a two-capitation record: the axes move independently (register D), the seam runs through the emergency room at 2 a.m. (register E), and only one of the two lets go at Medicare (register F).

What the File Says

Utah really does speak X12N 834 — two roster streams: one to the ACOs, one to the county behavioral authorities. Pick a member and read the transaction the receiver would get for that member's most recent event, segment by segment, with a plain-language note on every line.

In plain EnglishAn 834 is the state telling a payer: this person is yours now (or isn't anymore) — and in a carve-out state the same member generates that sentence twice, to two different receivers, meaning two different things. To the ACO: yours for physical health. To the county authority: yours for behavioral health. The two streams disagree on purpose — register D’s county movers appear as a change in one stream and nothing in the other; register F’s duals appear as a termination in the ACO stream while the county stream stays silent, because the carve-out does not read the Medicare flag. Reconciling two rosters that are both correct and deliberately different is the daily craft of carve-out states.
Members are grouped by register; the transaction is rebuilt live from the member's actual spans.

Utah Medicaid Eligibility Central — a single-file demonstration app in the Central series. All members, identifiers, addresses, phone numbers, and dates are synthetic and deterministic (seeded PRNG, seed 801001); any resemblance to real persons is coincidental. Program facts reflect public Utah Department of Health and Human Services policy of the mid-2020s and change year to year — the ACO service-area map, county authority roster, and expansion chronology are teaching simplifications; verify against current DHHS and CMS publications before any non-demonstration use.
