In plain EnglishEvery person here has a thirteen-digit Alabama Medicaid recipient ID that never changes — and one glance at the delivery column tells you what kind of app this is: it says the same thing on every row. Alabama is the last major all-fee-for-service state — no comprehensive managed care, ever — which makes this the control group of the entire series: eighteen apps of plans, capitations, and hand-offs, and here is the state where none of it happened. The texture moved elsewhere: into the coverage groups (watch PF01, QMB1, and SL02 — three chips whose spans promise far less than they appear to), and into an attribution overlay called the ACHN.

Eligibility Spans

One row per coverage-group span — the state's answer to “from when to when, and under which door did this person enter?” Click a row to open the member.

In plain EnglishA span is a promise with dates on it — and Alabama’s file is the series’ sharpest lesson that the promise varies wildly while the span looks the same. A CH19 span promises full benefits. A PF01 span promises one service line — family planning and nothing else. A QMB1 span promises no services at all: Medicaid pays Medicare premiums and cost sharing on the member’s behalf. An SL02 span promises exactly one monthly premium payment. Four open spans, identical in shape, spanning the distance from comprehensive coverage to a single wire transfer — the whole argument for a benefit-package column, in one roster.

Fee-for-Service & the ACHN

One row per delivery span — and every one of them is fee-for-service, because that is every delivery system Alabama has. The notes carry the actual texture: nursing facility vs. community, full benefits vs. Plan First’s single service line, and which of the seven ACHN networks holds the coordination attribution. Click a row to open the member.

In plain EnglishAlabama almost joined the series’ other eighteen states. From 2013 to 2017 it built Regional Care Organizations — provider-led, regionally capitated, legislated, certified, funded, staffed — and then canceled the entire reform before a single member-month was ever paid. Register D’s ghosts still carry probationary RCO assignment codes from 2016 in their archives: schemas that outlived the policy, a trading-partner directory with entries that never saw production traffic. What shipped instead, in October 2019, was the ACHN: seven regional care-coordination networks earning per-member-month fees over an untouched fee-for-service spine — attribution without capitation, the lightest delivery structure in this series. The claims never moved. In Alabama, the claims have never moved.

What the File Says

Alabama speaks X12N 834 too — not to health plans, but as attribution rosters to the seven ACHN networks, whose relationship to the member is a coordination fee rather than a capitation. Pick a member and read the file, segment by segment, with a plain-language note on every line.

In plain EnglishAn 834 is usually the state telling a health plan: this person is yours now. Alabama’s version means something lighter — this person is yours to coordinate — because the receiving ACHN holds a fee, not risk: the same grammar carrying a categorically smaller promise, which is this state’s theme in miniature. Register F’s MSP members have no plan file at all; their real EDI life is the state buy-in exchange with CMS — a premium-payment data flow, rendered below in 834 shape for teaching. And somewhere in the agency’s archive sit seven RCO trading-partner configurations, tested and certified in 2016, that never received a production file. The quietest folder in Alabama.
