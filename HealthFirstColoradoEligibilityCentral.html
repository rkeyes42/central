Members

The full synthetic roster. Click any row for the member record; click a column header to sort.

In plain EnglishEvery person here has a seven-character State ID — one letter, six digits — that never changes. The roster’s delivery column looks like other states’ at a glance, but it means something different: in Colorado nobody enrolled with those entities. They were attributed — assigned to a Regional Accountable Entity by their address, automatically, with no packet and no choice period. Change the address and the delivery answer changes with it. The only exceptions are the two purple-chip enclaves: Denver Health Medicaid Choice and RMHP Prime, real capitated MCOs that members actually join.

Eligibility Spans

One row per coverage-group span — the state's answer to “from when to when, and under which door did this person enter?” Click a row to open the member.

In plain EnglishA span is a promise with dates on it — determined in CBMS, applied for through PEAK, and closed for a reason. Colorado expanded in 2013 by statute, among the first states in, so the churn here has soft edges: an adult who loses a category usually lands in another one. What the eligibility side quietly powers is the delivery side — every open span below feeds the attribution engine that assigns the member’s RAE. Eligibility says yes; the address says where.

RAE Attribution

One row per attribution span: which Regional Accountable Entity held the member — assigned by residence — or one of the two enclave MCOs (Denver Health Medicaid Choice, RMHP Prime), or fee-for-service retro tails. Watch the June/July 2025 boundary: it runs through nearly every row. Click a row to open the member.

In plain EnglishColorado runs the country’s clearest counterexample to plan enrollment. Under the Accountable Care Collaborative, every member is attributed to a Regional Accountable Entity by residence — and the RAE is two things at once: a care coordinator sitting on top of fee-for-service physical health, and a capitated payer for the behavioral health benefit. One entity, two payment systems, same member. Then, on July 1, 2025, the ACC’s Phase III re-procurement went live and the whole state was re-attributed on a single day — the seam runs through nearly every timeline in this file, and for Weld, Larimer, and Boulder counties it changed the entity itself. Nobody chose anything; that is the point. The exceptions prove the rule: Denver Health Medicaid Choice and RMHP Prime, two true capitated MCOs living inside the attribution model, whose members’ spans sail across the seam untouched.

What the File Says

Colorado really does speak X12N 834 — HCPF’s attribution rosters to the RAEs ride the same standard every enrollment state uses, which is itself a teaching: the 834 doesn’t know whether the member chose. Pick a member and read the transaction, segment by segment, with a plain-language note on every line.

In plain EnglishAn 834 is the state telling an entity: this person is yours now (or isn't anymore) — and in Colorado “yours” means yours to coordinate, and yours to capitate for behavioral health, while physical health keeps paying FFS underneath. Two transactions here deserve a long look. The July 1, 2025 wave: the Phase III re-procurement re-attributed the entire state in one cycle — the largest single-day file this program ever cut, and every segment of it says 021 as if each member were new. And register D’s address-triggered pairs: a CBMS change-of-address that re-runs attribution and produces a 024/021 pair no member asked for. In most states an 834 records a decision. In Colorado it records geography.
Members are grouped by register; the transaction is rebuilt live from the member's actual spans.
